# Take it Back to Your Team

A short pack to use after the build sprint. The point is not to turn you into a developer overnight. It is to give you something useful to bring into the next conversation with your team, your supervisor, or your practice group, so the sprint does not stop at the demo.

Read it on the way home. Keep it open when you have your next team catch-up.

---

## What you actually did

Over a day or two, with no coding background, you built a working app yourself using an AI coding agent. You drove it. You described what you wanted, looked at what came back, said what was wrong, and iterated, again and again, until it did what you imagined. That is vibe coding.

The thing on the screen at the end is real. It runs in a browser. It does what you told it to do. It is not a mock-up, it is software.

That is the point you need to make first when you tell people about it. People will assume you watched a demo, or that someone built it for you. Neither is true. You built something.

---

## How to explain it in 60 seconds

If a manager or colleague asks you what the day was about, here is the version that lands:

> "We spent a couple of days using AI coding tools to build working internal applications. Each of us started with a real problem and, driving the AI agent ourselves, ended with a working web app and a short pitch. None of us had coded before. The point was not to learn programming. It was to see how quickly an idea can become a thing you can click on, and to start spotting where this could be useful in our actual work."

Three things to land:
1. We built, we did not watch, and nobody coded it for us
2. It went from blank page to working tool in a day or two
3. The constraint was the idea, not the technology

## What to bring into your next team conversation

Pick one or two of these depending on the team and the moment.

**The "what would you build?" question.** Ask your team what they would build if a working tool took a day or two instead of a quarter. The answers tell you what they find painful. That list is gold. Write the answers down.

**The "what do we redo every time?" question.** Vibe coding is best on small, repetitive, specific problems. Standard document generators. Checklist and tracker tools. First-pass document checkers. Knowledge lookups. Things people in the team build a spreadsheet or template for, again and again. Look for those.

**The "what would a new starter do?" question.** A lot of routine work is process-heavy: extracting points from documents, summarising, comparing, reformatting, drafting standard language. These are exactly the tasks where a small tool can save someone a half-day a week. Spot them.

**The honest version of the sprint.** Tell them what worked and what did not. The app probably broke at some point. You probably had to rephrase something five times, and reverting a bad change is part of the rhythm. That is part of the story. Do not oversell.

## What to say when someone asks "should we use this for real or sensitive work?"

Short answer: not yet, and not from your laptop.

Longer answer: what you built runs in a browser, was made in a day or two, and has not been through any security review. That is fine for a prototype. It is not fine for confidential or sensitive data. The path from "prototype" to "thing the organisation uses" goes through the right internal team, a security review, and a data classification check. See `SHIPPING.md` and your organisation's data and security policies for the full picture.

If someone in your team has an idea and wants to do something with it, the answer is "let me get the right people involved" and not "I will knock something up tonight."

## What you can and cannot do yourself, day to day

**You can:**
- Use your organisation's approved AI tools for non-confidential tasks (drafting, summarising, brainstorming, learning)
- Use approved tools on the work and data they have been cleared for
- Use approved productivity tools for everyday tasks (drafting emails, summarising threads, working in documents and spreadsheets)
- Suggest ideas to your manager and to the innovation or IT team
- Spot patterns in your team's work that look like prototype candidates

**You should not:**
- Build prototypes for live, sensitive work on personal devices
- Put confidential data into tools that have not been approved for it
- Skip your organisation's data classification process because "it is just a prototype"
- Promise someone that you can build a production tool for them this week (route it through the right team)

Your organisation's AI and acceptable-use policies are the authoritative versions of this. Read them if you have not.

## How to take an idea further

If your team has an idea worth taking further, the route is:

1. Write a one-paragraph description: who is the user, what problem does it solve, what would the tool do, what data does it touch
2. Send it to your organisation's innovation or IT team, or to the organisers (any of the people who ran the sprint, or your usual contact)
3. They will tell you whether it is something they can prototype, whether it is already covered by an existing tool, or whether it needs a different route

Do not worry about whether the idea is "big enough." Most of the highest-value tools start as a one-line frustration from someone close to the work.

---

## Three things worth doing this week

1. **Write down the three pain points your team complains about most.** Not the strategic ones. The boring, repetitive ones. The "we always have to redo this" ones.
2. **Mention the sprint in your next team meeting.** Two minutes, not ten. Use the 60-second version above.
3. **Pick one task you do this week and ask: could this have been a small app I built in a day?** You do not need to build it. Just notice it.

The build sprint was the start, not the end. The most valuable thing you can do now is bring what you built back to the people you work with, in plain language, with one or two ideas worth exploring.
