# The Plan

Vibe Coding Build Sprint - your one-page guide to the two days.

Over roughly 24 hours, from the morning of day 1 to the afternoon of day 2, you'll turn an idea in your head into working software, using an AI coding agent. You drive it yourself. Mentors are around on day 1 for advice, but nobody runs your build for you. On the afternoon of day 2, anyone who wants to can present what they built.

*Exact times and the room are on the screen on the day.*

---

## Day 1 morning - together

We start in the room as a group: [start time], [venue].

- A couple of short talks on what vibe coding is and how to get the most out of an AI coding agent.
- Live demos so you can see the workflow before you try it.
- Mentors on hand to talk through ideas and help you get set up.
- **You pick your idea.** Settle on one real, specific problem worth building. By the end of the morning you should know what you're making.

This starter repo is an optional kickstart. Clone it and you get a styled page and a `/kickoff` command to structure your plan, or start from scratch if you prefer.

---

## The build window - roughly 24 hours

This is the heart of it. From the end of the day 1 talks through to the day 2 presentations, you build.

- **You drive the agent.** Use Claude Code or ChatGPT Codex. Describe what you want, look at what comes back, say what's wrong, repeat.
- **Mentors are around on day 1** if you get stuck or want a second opinion. They advise; they don't take the keyboard.
- **It's your pace.** Keep going into the evening if you're enjoying it, the overnight is yours if you want it. There's no rule that says you have to.
- **Save your progress as you go.** Use `/handover` to close a session cleanly and `/pickup` to carry on in a fresh one, so you can stop and start across the two days without losing context.

You have real time here, so you can be more ambitious than a single tiny feature. Build something you'd be happy to show.

---

## Day 2 afternoon - present back

We come back together: [time], [venue]. Anyone who wants to shows what they built. A few minutes each, live.

---

## What makes a good idea

**Real** - something you've actually experienced, not theoretical. **Specific** - "people waste time copying the same numbers between two systems" is a problem; "our processes are inefficient" isn't. **Contained** - one focused tool, not a whole platform. You have time over 24 hours, so aim higher than one tiny feature, but keep the core sharp and buildable.

Starting question: *"What's the most annoying thing I do repeatedly that a computer should probably be doing?"*

---

## If you present (a few minutes, live)

1. **The problem** - who has it and why it matters.
2. **What you built** - one sentence, then a live demo. This is the centrepiece.
3. **What it would take to ship** - an honest line on what production would involve.
4. **The ask** - the next step if you took it further: a pilot, a decision, a sponsor.

Run `/onepager` for a fast one-page summary or `/present` for a fuller slide deck, and it structures this for you.

---

## Don't worry about

- Whether you understand the code. You don't need to.
- Whether it's production-ready. A working prototype is the goal; the path from prototype to pilot is a separate conversation, not a failure.

## One thing to do before you start

Think about your last few weeks. What took longer than it should have? What did you do by hand that a computer could have handled? What did you have to chase down that should have been easy to find? That's probably your app idea.
