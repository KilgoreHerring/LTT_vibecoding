# What You Need to Know Before We Start

Welcome to the build sprint. You're not here to become a developer.

You're here to find out what becomes possible when you can describe an idea clearly enough for an AI to build it - and then drive that build yourself.

---

## What Is Vibe Coding?

Vibe coding means building a real, working piece of software by describing what you want in plain English - no programming knowledge required.

You tell an AI coding agent (Claude Code or ChatGPT Codex) what you want to build, what it should do, and who it's for. The agent writes the code. You test it, give feedback, and keep refining until it does what you imagined.

The skill is thinking clearly about a problem and communicating it precisely. Clear thinkers tend to be good at this. You don't need to know how the code works - you need to know what you want and be able to say it plainly.

---

## How This Works

Day 1 starts with everyone together: a couple of short talks, some demos, and mentors on hand for advice. You'll pick an idea. Then you go off and build it yourself, driving an AI coding agent over roughly the next day. Day 2 afternoon, you present what you built back to the room.

The build window is about 24 hours across the two days - the morning of day 1 through to the afternoon of day 2, with the evening in between if you want it. That's a lot of time. With a day or so of focused work and an AI agent doing the typing, you can build something genuinely useful, not just a toy.

The shape of it:

- **Day 1 morning:** talks, demos, pick your idea, plan it, start building.
- **Through day 1 and into day 2:** build, test, and iterate. Get something working end to end early, then build out.
- **Day 2 afternoon:** polish, prepare your demo, and present.

Mentors are around on day 1 if you want a second opinion on your idea, your scope, or a problem you've hit. They're there for advice. The build is yours.

---

## You Drive This

You come up with the idea, define the problem, describe what you want, test what comes back, and refine it. You run the AI coding agent yourself. Nobody types the prompts for you - that's the whole point, and it's more approachable than it sounds. You talk to the agent in plain English and it does the work.

The thinking is the work. The agent handles the rest.

---

## What Makes a Good Idea

The best ideas have three things in common. They're real: something you've actually experienced, not theoretical. They're specific: "people waste time chasing status updates by email" is a problem, "our processes are inefficient" isn't. And they have a clear, contained core: one thing the tool does well that you can get working first.

With around 24 hours you can be more ambitious than a single tiny feature. The trick is to scope a minimum viable version first - the smallest thing that actually works end to end - get it running, then build out from there in phases. Start small so you have something real early. Then layer on more once the core holds together.

The themes to spark ideas:

- Repetitive tasks: the things you do over and over in your daily work
- Onboarding and learning: helping people get up to speed faster
- Finding and surfacing information: making knowledge easier to locate and use
- Working with documents or data: tidying, summarising, comparing, extracting
- Relationships and stakeholders: staying on top of who you need to keep close

You don't have to stay strictly within a theme. If you have a better idea, run with it.

Start by asking yourself: *"What's the most annoying thing I do repeatedly at work that a computer should probably be doing?"*

---

## What You'll Present at the End

On day 2 afternoon you'll present what you built. You'll cover:

1. **The problem** - who has it and why it matters
2. **What you built** - what it does in one sentence, then a live demo
3. **What it would take to ship** - an honest look at what getting this into production would actually involve
4. **The ask** - if you were taking this further, what's the next step?

When you're prepping your demo, run `/onepager` and you'll get a one-page summary generated automatically to help structure this (or `/present` for a fuller slide deck). The demo is the centrepiece - build something you can actually show working.

---

## What You Don't Need to Worry About

- Whether you understand the code - you don't need to
- Whether your idea is too simple - simple and working beats complex and broken, and you can always build out from a solid core
- Whether it would actually pass an IT review today - the shipping checklist covers that at the end

---

## One Thing to Do Before We Start

Think about your last few weeks at work. What took longer than it should have? What did you do manually that felt like something a computer could handle? What information did you have to chase down that should have been easy to find?

That's probably your app idea.
